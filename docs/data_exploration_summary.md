# CUD / RTA Traffic Dataset — Data Exploration Summary

_Generated by an automated profiling pass over all CSVs in `CUD_RTA_Traffic_Dataset/datasets`._

> **⭐ Recommended angle — Predictive analytics (demand & congestion forecasting), feeding signal-timing optimization.**
>
> The data is purpose-built for forecasting: **three complete, gap-free, duplicate-free years of hourly data** (2023-01-01 → 2025-12-31) across traffic volume, signal performance, Salik tolls and weather, plus daily metro ridership and a rich daily calendar — joined by stable keys with **100% referential integrity (0 orphans)**. Clear prediction targets (`volume_vph`, `demand_vph`, `avg_speed_kph`, `travel_time_index`, `vc_ratio`, Salik `crossings`, metro `ridership`) come bundled with the exogenous drivers needed to predict them (hourly weather, holiday/Ramadan/DSF/school flags, an incident log). **Adaptive signal control** is a strong secondary angle — the signal tables are unusually detailed — but is bounded to **10 junctions at hourly resolution** (no per-cycle data). **Smart routing** is the weakest fit: there is no road-network graph or origin-destination matrix, only isolated per-link metrics.
>
> | Direction | Fit | Why (one line, based on what the data actually allows) |
> |---|---|---|
> | **Predictive analytics** | ⭐⭐⭐ Best | 3 yrs × clean hourly panel with explicit targets **and** weather/calendar/incident drivers + perfect join keys. |
> | **Adaptive signal control** | ⭐⭐ Strong (bounded) | Rich `signal_performance` + per-phase `signal_timing_plans`, but only 10 junctions, hourly (not per-cycle). |
> | **Smart routing** | ⭐ Weak | Per-link speed/TTI + lat/long exist, but no network topology / adjacency / OD demand to route on. |
>
> Detailed scoring & evidence in **§8**. Supporting facts: **§2** (join keys), **§3** (location coverage), **§6** (depth), **§7** (quality).

## 8. Q7 — Which RTA challenge direction does the data best support?

Scored against **what the data actually allows**, not what's conceptually appealing. (Compact table is in the Recommended-angle box at the top.)

### 🥇 Predictive analytics — best fit
**Evidence.** Three complete years of hourly observations — ≈473K traffic-volume rows/yr, ≈87K signal rows/yr, ≈61K Salik rows/yr, ≈8.76K weather hours/yr — with **zero missing timestamps and zero duplicates**. Ready-made prediction targets at multiple layers: corridor `volume_vph`/`demand_vph`/`avg_speed_kph`/`travel_time_index`/`vc_ratio` (18 locations), junction `degree_of_saturation`/delay/queue (10 junctions), Salik `crossings`/`revenue_aed` (7 gates), daily metro `ridership` (2 lines). Exogenous drivers are unusually complete: hourly weather, a daily calendar (public holidays, Ramadan, DSF, school status, rain/dust severity), and a geolocated incident log — all joinable on stable keys.

**Why it wins.** Everything a supervised forecaster needs — target + temporally-aligned drivers + 3-year history + clean keys — is present and pre-joined by `datetime`/`date` + `location_id`/`junction_id`. Supports hour-ahead and day-ahead forecasting, incident-impact estimation, and demand profiling out of the box.

**Gaps.** None blocking; the main limit is spatial breadth (18 corridors / 10 junctions, not a city-wide grid).

### 🥈 Adaptive signal control — strong but bounded
**Evidence.** `signal_performance_hourly` carries the exact control-relevant signals: `degree_of_saturation`, `avg_delay_s_per_veh`, `avg_queue_veh`, `throughput_vph`, `phase_failures`, `pedestrian_calls`, `cycle_length_s`, `active_program`, and an `adaptive_active` flag (≈60% of junction-hours already adaptive). `signal_timing_plans` gives the full **per-phase** plan — green/yellow/all-red, min/max green, cycle length, coordination offset, movement — for each of the 10 junctions, with `control_type` (SCOOT-adaptive vs Fixed-time). `approach_demand_vph` supplies the load.

**Why bounded.** Data is **hourly-aggregated for only 10 junctions** — there is no per-cycle / sub-minute arrival or phase-actuation data, and no junction-to-junction network. So you can do **offline timing-plan optimization, saturation/delay prediction, and adaptive-vs-fixed benefit analysis**, but not true real-time closed-loop control or microscopic simulation from this data alone.

**Best use.** Pair it with the forecaster: predict saturation/delay → recommend cycle/green-split or adaptive-mode changes; quantify the delay reduction of SCOOT-adaptive vs Fixed-time across demand/weather conditions.

### 🥉 Smart routing — weakest fit
**Evidence for inputs.** Per-link hourly `avg_speed_kph`, `travel_time_index`, `vc_ratio`, `level_of_service` for 18 directional corridors (each geolocated), plus 7 Salik gates — good *edge-cost* signals.

**Why weak.** There is **no network topology**: no link adjacency, no node/intersection connectivity between the 18 corridors, no turn movements, and no origin-destination matrix or trip data. The 18 traffic locations and 10 signal junctions are also disjoint point sets (§3). Routing needs a graph to search; this dataset supplies candidate edge weights but neither the graph nor the demand — so it cannot support routing without external map/OD data (e.g. an OpenStreetMap network + an OD model).
